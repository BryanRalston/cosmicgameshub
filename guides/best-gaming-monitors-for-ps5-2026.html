Best Gaming Monitors for PS5 2026: Top 5 Picks for 4K 120fps

PS5 can output 4K at 120fps — but only if your monitor has HDMI 2.1. Most gaming monitors still ship with HDMI 2.0, which caps you at 4K@60fps or 1080p@120fps. In 2026, the monitors that truly unlock PS5's performance range from $299 (1440p with HDMI 2.1) to $799 (OLED 4K with Dolby Vision). We've picked the five best options — including the HDMI 2.1 explainer you need before buying anything.

HDMI 2.1 vs HDMI 2.0: Why It Matters for PS5

HDMI 2.1 delivers 48 Gbps of bandwidth — four times the 18 Gbps of HDMI 2.0. That extra bandwidth is what makes 4K@120fps possible. The math:

  • 4K@60fps (HDMI 2.0 max): ~17.8 Gbps — fits within the 18 Gbps HDMI 2.0 ceiling
  • 4K@120fps (requires HDMI 2.1): ~35.6 Gbps — requires 2.1's 48 Gbps headroom
  • 1440p@120fps (HDMI 2.0): ~18.8 Gbps — technically possible but right at the limit; may require Display Stream Compression
  • 1080p@120fps (HDMI 2.0): ~8.9 Gbps — fully supported, no issue

What to check: Look for "HDMI 2.1" listed in the monitor's port specs — not just "supports 4K@120Hz." Some monitors advertise 4K@120Hz via DisplayPort (fine for PC) but only have HDMI 2.0 ports, which won't give you 4K@120Hz from PS5. The PS5 has one HDMI 2.1 port and that's what you're connecting. The monitors in this guide all specify their HDMI version clearly.

VRR and ALLM on PS5: What They Do

Variable Refresh Rate (VRR)

PS5 added VRR support in a 2022 firmware update. When VRR is active, the monitor's refresh rate dynamically matches the PS5's frame output — eliminating screen tearing without the input lag introduced by traditional V-Sync. If a game drops from 120fps to 95fps during a complex scene, the monitor adjusts to 95Hz instead of introducing judder. VRR on PS5 works via HDMI 2.1's built-in VRR spec (different from AMD FreeSync or Nvidia G-Sync, but compatible with monitors that support HDMI Forum VRR or have FreeSync Premium Pro certification). All monitors in this guide support VRR with PS5.

Auto Low Latency Mode (ALLM)

ALLM automatically switches a monitor into its lowest input-lag "game mode" when a PS5 game is detected — no manual menu diving required. Without ALLM, some monitors default to post-processing modes (with added sharpening, motion smoothing, etc.) that add 20–50ms of input lag. ALLM ensures you're always in the lowest-latency mode when gaming. Look for "ALLM" or "Auto Game Mode" in the monitor's spec sheet. All top picks in this guide include it.

PS5 HDR Output: OLED vs LCD

PS5 outputs HDR10 and Dolby Vision (on supported displays). On paper, HDR transforms the image — brighter highlights, deeper shadows, wider color gamut. In practice, the quality depends almost entirely on the monitor's hardware:

  • HDR400 (most LCD monitors): 400 nits peak brightness, no local dimming. Minimal visible improvement over SDR. Marketing more than performance.
  • HDR600 (LG 27GP950-B): 600 nits, some local dimming zones. Noticeable real-world HDR with brighter highlights and better contrast.
  • OLED (LG C3): Each pixel is its own light source. True black (pixel turns off). 1,000+ nit highlights. No halo around bright objects. Dolby Vision support. This is what PS5's HDR output was designed for.

Honest verdict: If HDR quality matters to you, OLED is categorically better than any LCD at any price. The LG C3 27" OLED at $799 produces HDR images that LCD panels at $399 cannot match — not because the LCDs are bad, but because OLED's pixel-level control is physically different. For most gamers, the LG 27GP950-B's HDR600 is excellent value. The OLED jump costs $400 more and delivers an experience that's visually in a different class.
